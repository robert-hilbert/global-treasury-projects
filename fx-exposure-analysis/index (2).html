01

Core FX driver: rate discrepancy

IC Sale
Entity 1420 (EUR) sells to Poland LRD (PLN)
Intercompany sale originates from EUR-functional entity 1420. The receiving entity in Poland operates in PLN. This cross-currency IC transaction is the root of the FX exposure chain.
Click to expand →
Invoice pricing
Set in PLN using plan rate
The invoice to Poland is priced in PLN at the budgeted/plan FX rate established at the beginning of the fiscal year. This rate is static and does not move with market fluctuations.
Click to expand →
1420 Accounting
Converted to EUR using daily rate
Entity 1420 records the sale in EUR using the prevailing daily spot rate (or monthly average). This rate fluctuates with the market, creating a variance against the plan rate used for pricing.
Click to expand →
Decision
THE DELTA: Is there variance between plan rate and daily rate?
When the PLN plan rate used for invoicing diverges from the actual EUR/PLN daily rate used for 1420's books, a rate discrepancy emerges. The magnitude of this delta directly drives the FX exposure.
Click to expand →
Yes
Exposure created
FX EXPOSURE CREATED
The delta between plan rate and daily rate creates a realized FX exposure on every intercompany transaction. Over a full period with thousands of IC invoices, this compounds into a material portfolio-level impact.
Click to expand →
Consolidation
Intercompany sales eliminate on consolidation
During consolidation, the IC sale and corresponding IC purchase eliminate. However, the FX variance between the two sides of the elimination does not net to zero — it persists as a residual FX impact.
Click to expand →
Margin impact
FX difference "leaks" through to consolidated margin
The FX variance that isn't eliminated leaks through to the consolidated P&L as a margin impact. This is the core mechanism by which the EUR/PLN rate discrepancy affects reported financial results.

Accounting treatment & strategy

driven by margin impact (column 1)
Current state
Hedge impacts posted to revenue
Today, the FX hedge gains/losses from the derivative portfolio are posted to the revenue line on the P&L. This treatment has been consistent historically but may not accurately reflect the economic driver of the exposure.

Proposed shift
Move hedge impacts to COGS (driven by EUR cost basis)
The rationale: if the underlying exposure is driven by the EUR cost of goods flowing through intercompany, the hedge impact should logically sit in COGS, not revenue. This would better align hedge accounting with the economic substance of the exposure.
